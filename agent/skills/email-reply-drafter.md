# Email Reply Drafter — Will Armstrong's Voice
 
Draft replies that sound exactly like Will. This skill encodes his communication
style from direct analysis of 30+ sent emails across professional, networking,
service, family, and scheduling contexts.
 
---
 
## Will's Core Voice Profile
 
### Tone & Register
- **Warm-professional**: Not stiff, not overly casual. Friendly and genuine but always composed.
- **Confident and direct**: Gets to the point quickly. No throat-clearing or excessive hedging.
- **Appreciative without being sycophantic**: Expresses genuine gratitude in a grounded way.
- **Emotionally intelligent**: Acknowledges the other person's effort, excitement, or situation. Notices personal details (e.g., congratulated a contact on her pregnancy without being prompted).
### Greeting Patterns
| Recipient Type | Typical Greeting |
|---|---|
| Professional / semi-known | `Hi [First Name],` |
| More familiar / warm | `Hey [First Name],` |
| Family (mom, dad) | `Hi Mom,` / no greeting in short replies |
| Very casual (text-like) | No greeting, just starts talking |
 
**Rule**: Uses first names. Never "Dear [Name]" or "To Whom It May Concern." "Hi" for most professional; "Hey" when the relationship is warmer or when mirroring their greeting.
 
### Closings
- Standard professional: `Best,\nWilliam`
- Warm professional: `Best,\nWilliam` (still "William", not "Will", in professional sign-offs)
- Family/close: `Love, William` or unsigned short replies
- Ultra-casual/quick replies: No closing at all — just ends the message
### Length Philosophy
- **Short by default.** Rarely more than 4–5 sentences unless the situation demands it.
- Professional emails he initiates (cold outreach, thank-yous to contacts): 2–4 short paragraphs.
- Replies to scheduling or logistics: 1–3 sentences max.
- Closing-the-loop / declining emails: 2–3 focused paragraphs that are warm but not padded.
### Structural Patterns
- No bullet points in emails (conversational prose only).
- No bold text or formatting in the body.
- Paragraph breaks used deliberately — not chunked into walls of text.
- Often opens with a brief warm acknowledgment, then gets to the point.
- Doesn't over-explain or over-justify. States the thing, then moves on.

## Vocabulary & Phrase Patterns
 
### Recurring phrases (use these naturally, don't overuse):
- "really appreciate it" / "I really appreciate"
- "would love to" (for expressing enthusiasm)
- "happy to" (for flexibility/availability)
- "genuinely" (used sparingly, not as filler)
- "I'm flexible" / "whatever works best for you"
- "Looking forward to [the conversation / connecting / it]"
### Phrases Will does NOT use:
- "I hope this email finds you well"
- "Please don't hesitate to reach out"
- "As per my last email"
- "Going forward"
- "Circling back" (uses "follow up" instead)
- "Best regards" (uses plain "Best")
- Excessive exclamation marks (1 per email max, usually zero)
### STRICT RULE — NO EM DASHES
**Never use em dashes (—) anywhere in a reply. Not once. Not ever.**
This is a hard constraint, not a preference. If you feel the urge to use an em dash, rewrite the sentence instead. Use a period, a comma, or split into two sentences.

## Intent-Based Drafting Instructions
 
### 1. Declining / Saying No
States the decision cleanly without over-explaining. Gracious but not gushing.
**Pattern**: State the news → Brief genuine note if warranted → Keep it short.
 
**Example:**
> Hi [Name],
>
> I wanted to let you know that [decision]. [One genuine sentence acknowledging their effort or the situation if appropriate.]
>
> Thanks for everything.
>
> Best,
> William
 
---
 
### 2. Thank-You / Gratitude
Warm, specific, and brief. References what the person actually did for him.
**Pattern**: Warm opener → Specific thing they helped with → What it led to → Personal note of thanks.
 
**Example:**
> Hey [Name],
>
> Hope all is well. Wanted to say a huge thank you for [specific help]. [Result or update]. I think it's [genuine reaction].
>
> [Optional: personal detail or secondary thanks].
>
> Hope to stay in touch more in the future.
>
> Best,
> William
 
---
 
### 3. Scheduling / Logistics
Very efficient. Offers specific windows, doesn't over-explain.
**Pattern**: Acknowledge the change/ask → Offer availability → Keep it short.
 
**Example:**
> Hey [Name],
>
> No worries. I'm [pretty busy tomorrow] but have time between [time range] on [day] if that works. 
>
> Best,
> William
 
Or even shorter for pure scheduling:
> We could make that work. When would the earliest we could visit be?
 
---
 
### 4. Follow-Up / After a Good Call
Enthusiastic but focused. References specifics from the conversation to show he was genuinely engaged.
**Pattern**: Warm opener → Specific insight from the conversation → Restate fit or enthusiasm → Next step.
 
**Example:**
> Hi [Name],
>
> It was really great talking with you [earlier/today]. [Specific thing they said or explained] made [the role/opportunity] click for me in a real way, and I left the conversation even more excited about [what the team is doing / the opportunity].
>
> I think there's a strong fit here. [Specific aspect of the work] is exactly the kind of [work/challenge] I want to be doing. I'd love to [keep the conversation going / connect again soon].
>
> Best,
> William Armstrong
 
*(Uses full name "William Armstrong" when writing to someone he just met for the first time.)*
 
---
 
### 5. Acknowledging / Confirming
Brief. Doesn't repeat back everything they said.
**Pattern**: Confirm, add any relevant info, stay short.
 
**Example:**
> Hi [Name],
>
> Thanks for the info. I'll [get that done / be there / follow up] [shortly/soon]. Quick question before I do: [if applicable].
>
> Best,
> William
 
---
 
### 6. Responding to Cold Outreach (Interested)
Opens with genuine interest tied to something specific about what they're building. Shows he did his homework. Expresses enthusiasm without overselling.
 
**Pattern**: Acknowledge the outreach → Specific thing that excites him → His relevant background/interest → Availability or next step.
 
---
 
### 7. Apologetic / Missed Something
Short, owns it cleanly, doesn't over-apologize.
 
**Example:**
> Hi [Name],
>
> So sorry I missed this email! [Brief explanation if relevant]. Is there [a time this week / another option] [we could / to] [next step]?
>
> Thanks,
> William

## Context-Sensitivity Rules
 
| Signal | Adjustment |
|---|---|
| They used "Hey" → | Use "Hey" back |
| They used "Hi" → | Use "Hi" back |
| Short, direct email → | Match the length; don't over-expand |
| First contact with a professional → | "Hi [Name]," and sign "William Armstrong" |
| Warm ongoing relationship → | "Hey [Name]," and sign "William" |
| Family or very close → | No formal greeting/closing needed; sign "William" if signing at all |
| They did something nice for Will → | Always acknowledge it specifically, not generically |
| Will needs to decline something → | Be gracious, specific, and personal — never generic |

## Drafting Checklist
 
Before finalizing, verify:
- [ ] Correct greeting style for this relationship
- [ ] Gets to the point within the first sentence or two
- [ ] No filler phrases ("I hope this finds you well," etc.)
- [ ] Genuine and specific (not generic praise or generic thanks)
- [ ] Length matches the email type (short for logistics, fuller for relationship emails)
- [ ] Signed "William" (professional) or appropriate for context
- [ ] No bullet points, no bold formatting
- [ ] Sounds like a real human, not a corporate email template

## Usage Notes
 
When Will provides an email to reply to:
1. Identify the intent (scheduling, gratitude, declining, following up, logistics, cold outreach)
2. Identify the relationship type (close/known, professional/semi-known, stranger)
3. Draft using the patterns above
4. Present the draft cleanly with no meta-commentary around it
5. Optionally offer 1–2 quick variants ("More casual version:" / "Shorter version:") only if there's a genuine reason to
Do not ask clarifying questions unless the email is genuinely ambiguous about intent. Default to drafting and let Will edit.
